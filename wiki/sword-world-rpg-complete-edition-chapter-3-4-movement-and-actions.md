## **Movement and Actions**

1 round lasts _10 seconds_. Therefore, a character can also move during this time. However, when **movement** is made, actions are restricted. You cannot wield weapons or cast spells while running at full speed.

Naturally, you can advance farther, but the restrictions on your actions will also be greater. At this time, attacks from the enemy are also much more likely to hit.

The distance you can go with full movement is **character's agility x 3** meters. For monsters, their **movement speed** is set separately from agility, so they can move up to **movement speed x 3** meters.

Characters have three options for movement: **full movement**, **normal movement**, or **stand still**. Each has different distances that can be traveled and different restrictions on actions that can be taken.

The following actions can be taken during full movement:

- Ready a sheathed weapon
- Switch a weapon from hand to hand
- Drop a weapon, etc. on the ground
- Sheath a weapon, etc.
- Withdraw (flee) from engagement

Below are some guidelines on how movement works and which actions can be taken. For any other actions, please use _common sense_ based on the time frame of 1 round = 10 seconds. Final decisions are to be made by the game master.

## **Actions You Can Take Regardless of Movement Method**

The following actions can be taken _at any time_ , regardless of the movement method. They do not prevent other actions at all.

- Drop a weapon, etc. on the ground
- Switch a weapon from hand to hand
- Pick a weapon up off the ground and ready it
- Switch out a one-handed weapon
- Ready a weapon and attack
- Switch a weapon's hands and attack
- Attack with a readied weapon

## **Actions You Can Take During Full Movement**

- Ready a sheathed weapon
- Switch a weapon from hand to hand
- Drop a weapon, etc. on the ground
- Sheath a weapon, etc.
- Withdraw (flee) from engagement

## **Actions You Can Take During Normal Movement**

This is movement while staying alert to your surroundings.

The distance you can move is a **number of meters equal to your character's agility** (or a monster's **movement speed**).

At this time, the following actions are possible:

- Ready a weapon
- Sheath a weapon, etc.
- Pick a weapon up off the ground and ready it
- Switch out a one-handed weapon
- Ready a weapon and attack
- Switch a weapon's hands and attack
- Attack with a readied weapon

## **Actions You Can Take While Standing Still**

Although stationary, you do not have to remain completely still; you can move up to **3 meters**.

In this state, all _actions you can take during normal movement_ described in the previous section can be performed.

The advantage of _standing still_ is the ability to use magic and attack with projectiles. Conversely, if you are not _standing still_ , you _cannot_ use magic or projectiles.

If you want to attack with a projectile, please follow the process of _actions you can take during normal movement_ . If you chose _stand still_ , then _attack with that weapon_ can be read as _attack with that projectile_ . However, when you're _engaged_ (being attacked by a melee weapon), you _cannot_ use projectiles.

If you choose to _stand still_ , in addition to magic and projectiles, you can also take the following special action:

- Stand up and ready yourself

## **Withdraw (Flee) From Engagement**

You can escape from an opponent within melee attack range.

If an attack is declared on a character who is withdrawing, another character is needed to suffer the enemy's attack in their place (defend against the attack).

If you do not have a companion to take on your opponent, you will escape with your back completely turned, and you will suffer a **-4 penalty** when evading the attack. Also, in that case, regardless of your character's agility, they will only escape at the _end_ of the round (agility 0, based on action order). In other words, there is _always_ a chance for the enemy to attack a character before they withdraw.

## **Ready a Sheathed Weapon**

You can wield up to two, one in each hand. If you want to switch your weapon from a projectile such as a bow, to a melee weapon, you can drop your previous weapon on the ground, and then you can ready a new weapon. This is because, from the beginning, it's assumed your hands were empty.

## **Switch a Weapon From Hand to Hand**

Weapons and shields can be dropped on the ground at _any time_ , regardless of whether they're one-handed, two-handed, or both-handed. If you do drop a weapon, etc. consider it as if you _never had it in the first place_ , then choose your action.

You can move something in your right hand to your left hand, or hold something you had in one hand with both hands.

Some weapons can be held in either one or both hands. If you have such a weapon in one hand and a shield in the other, if you drop the shield, the weapon is now considered to be in only one hand, so you can then hold it with both hands.

## **Sheath a Weapon, Etc.**

You can sheath up to two, one in each hand. However, this is only possible if there is a sheath, etc. You can also place a shield on your back.

## **Pick a Weapon Up off the Ground and Ready It**

If a weapon or shield has fallen on the ground, you can pick it up and ready it. However, that weapon _cannot_ be used to attack this round.

## **Switch Out a One-Handed Weapon**

Return a weapon in your hand to its sheath, etc. and ready a _different_ weapon. Only _one_ can be switched out. For example, if you have a sword and a shield, you _cannot_ return them both and ready a different weapon.

If you do have a sword and a shield, you can drop _one_ of them on the ground, return the _other_ one, then ready a different weapon. If you drop one of the things you were holding, it's as if you were only holding one from the beginning.

## **Ready a Weapon and Attack**

Take a weapon out of a sheath, etc. that you're wearing and use it to attack immediately. However, bows and other projectiles _cannot_ be used to attack while moving.

## **Switch a Weapon's Hands and Attack**

Switch a weapon you're using with only one hand to _both_ hands and attack. This action may be effective if you have a weapon that can be used with one or both hands.

If you're holding a weapon in one hand and a shield in the other, and that weapon can be used with both hands, you can drop the shield on the ground and attack with both hands, since it's as if you were holding the weapon in only one hand from the beginning.

## **Attack with a Readied Weapon**

If you already have a weapon ready, you can of course attack with that weapon. However, bows and other projectiles cannot be used to attack while moving.

## **Stand Up and Ready Yourself**

From a state of falling down, stand up and ready yourself. You can only ready _one_ weapon, etc. that you're wearing. You _cannot_ pick up any items on the ground.

## **Engaged with Attempt to Withdraw (Flee)**

Even if they have high agility, you can still attack, and you will have a higher chance of hitting than usual.

## **Chapter 4: Weapon Combat**

This section covers weapon and armor restrictions, combat skills, projectile restrictions, and scores used in weapon combat.

### **Weapon and Armor Restrictions**

A character's arms are largely divided into weapons, shields, and armor. Required strength should not be added up, but rather considered separately for each of these. In other words, a character with 14 strength can wield a weapon with a required strength of 14, wield a shield with a required strength of 14, and wear armor with a required strength of 14.

All joking aside, however, if we want to be realistic, weapons and armor used should be limited by a character's strength. All weapons and armor have a required strength to use them. Characters may not use weapons or armor with a required strength greater than their own strength.

Under the basic rules, a character can be armed with either one-handed weapon + shield + armor or two-handed weapon + armor. Of course, one-handed weapon + armor, two-handed weapon without armor, etc. are also possible, and it is acceptable to carry a spare weapon on your waist or back, as long as it is within the bounds of common sense. However, you may not hold two weapons in both hands or hold a shield in each hand-- it is fine to do so, but a shield in your dominant hand or a weapon in your non-dominant hand is of no use.

### **Weapon Combat Skills**

To be good at weapon combat, a character must have skills. The skills that can be used for this purpose are limited to the following three:

- **Fighter skill**
- **Thief skill**
- **Ranger skill**

### **Fighter Skill**

The fighter skill is the most basic skill for weapon combat. In the following explanation, we will focus on this skill.

### **Thief Skill**

A thief also has the ability to fight, but only with _light_ equipment. Specifically, you can only fight if the weapon or armor you're using requires up to _half (rounded up)_ of your character's strength. As for armor, it's limited not only by required strength but also by _type_ . Metal armor other than ring mail may _not_ be used, even if the required strength (half of strength) is met.

### **Ranger Skill**

The ranger skill is proficient with projectiles, and _only_ when using a weapon that is designated as such– _throwable_ , _made for throwing_ , or _shooting_ on Table 1-8: Weapons (see p. 21) - can they fight as well as a fighter when attacking. There is no ability to attack with melee weapons. Weapons that can be used for both melee _and_ throwing, as indicated by _throwable_ on the Weapons table, are useful _only_ when used for throwing, _not_ for melee. The ranger skill cannot be used for defense.

### **Projectile Restrictions**

There are some restrictions when handling projectiles such as bows and javelins.

If an enemy is beyond this distance, the projectile will _not_ hit them.

In addition, projectiles _cannot_ be used in melee. When there's an enemy in front of you, you can _never_ use a projectile marked _shooting_ or _made for throwing_ . Weapons marked as _throwable_ can _also_ be used as melee weapons, but _not_ as projectiles.

One is their **range** . There is no bow that has infinite reach. The maximum of shooting weapons such as bows and arrows– those marked _shooting_ on the Weapons table (see p. 21) --is **100m** , and of throwing weapons such as spears– those marked _throwable_ or _made for throwing_ on the Weapons table --is **20m** .

Again, movement is _also_ restricted when using projectiles. A character must choose _stand still_ as their movement method, and can only move up to _3 meters in 1 round_ .

### **Scores Used in Weapon Combat**

During weapon combat, each of the following scores are used:

- **Attack power**
- **Strike power**
- **Bonus damage**
- **Critical target**
- **Evasion speed**
- **Defense power**
- **Damage reduction**

#### **Attack Power**

This is the number needed to determine whether a character's attack will hit or not. The higher this score, the higher the probability that a character's attack will hit.

Attack power is calculated with the following formula:

**attack power** = fighter (thief/ranger) skill level + dexterity bonus

If fighter (or thief, ranger) skill level is _0_ , then attack power is **0** regardless of the character's dexterity bonus.

#### **Critical Target**

If a weapon hits, it'll sometimes deal more damage than normal. For example, when you successfully exploit a gap in your opponent's armor, or when you successfully damage an opponent's vital point, such as an eye.

In _Sword World RPG_ , this is resolved with a process called a **critical** . The **critical target** is the number needed to determine whether a critical occurs.

Usually the critical target is **10** . However, when attacking with the _thief_ skill, it is instead **9** .

**critical target** = 10 (9 if using thief skill)

#### **Strike Power**

This is the number needed to determine how much damage a character's attack will deal if it hits. The greater this score, the more damage a character will deal.

Strike power is the same number as the _required strength_ of the weapon used. However, if the weapon is a _two-handed_ weapon, it's instead _required strength + 5_ .

**strike power** = required strength of weapon (+5 if a two-handed weapon)

If a character does _not_ have a weapon (they're _barehanded_ ), Strike Power is **0** .

#### **Evasion Speed**

In combat, characters are not the only ones who can attack. Naturally, the enemy will also attack the characters. To evade an enemy's attack, a score called **evasion speed** is necessary.

A character with excellent evasion speed will be able to evade enemy attacks more easily. No matter how formidable the weapon, it's not _that_ scary if it doesn't hit.

Characters with deep experience in combat can handle weapons effectively. In addition, characters with high strength can wield weapons more powerfully. These characters can deal more damage to their foes, even when using the same sized weapon. This is represented by **bonus damage** .

**evasion speed** = fighter (thief) skill level + agility bonus

If fighter (or thief) skill level is _0_ , then evasion speed is **0** regardless of the character's agility bonus.

A character holding a _shield_ receives a +1 bonus to evasion speed. The size of the shield (whether it is a small or large shield) is completely irrelevant unless you introduce the optional rule _16.2: Weapon and Armor Modifiers_ (see p. 262). A fighter (or thief) skill level of 0 does _not_ prevent you from receiving this shield bonus. Therefore, the evasion speed of such a character will be **1** .

#### **Bonus Damage**

If fighter (or thief, ranger) skill level is _0_ , bonus damage is **0** regardless of the character's strength bonus.

Bonus damage is calculated with the following formula:

**bonus damage** = fighter (thief/ranger) skill level + strength bonus

#### **Defense Power**

If by a stroke of bad luck, an opponent's attack hits, a character can still reduce the damage by means of armor. It is **defense power** that represents this.

Defense power is the same number as the _required strength_ of the armor worn by the character.

**defense power** = required strength of armor

If a character is not wearing any armor (just clothes or nothing at all), then defense power is **0** .

#### **Damage Reduction**

Those who are called heroes do not die from just a few injuries. In _Sword World RPG_ , a character with a high adventurer level, that is, a character who has a high level in any adventurer skill, is considered a _hero_ . Therefore, we determine a hero's resistance to death by their _adventurer level_ .

**Damage reduction** is a score that reduces the damage a character would have taken. It's exactly equal to a character's adventurer level.

**damage reduction** = adventurer level

## **Filling out the Character Sheet**

Please write these scores in the designated fields on the Character Sheet. This will make the game run more smoothly.

The entry fields may seem complicated, but this is to assist you when using _Chapter 16: Optional Rules for Combat_ (see p. 260). Now, don't worry about it too much. It is sufficient to write down each weapon, shield, and armor along with their required strength, as well as attack power, strike power, critical target, bonus damage, evasion speed, defense power, and damage reduction in the _total_ field at the bottom.

Have you learned each of these terms? Now, let's get into the actual description of the combat procedure. Combat consists of two checks: one is made to determine if an attack _hits_ , and if the total result _is_ a hit, another is made to determine the _damage_ dealt by the attack.

These procedures differ when the attacker and defender are _character against monster_ , _monster against character_ , or _character against character_ . There is one other case, _monster against monster_ , but since only the game master should know how to handle this case, we won't explain it here.
