# The Antikythera Mechanism

The Antikythera mechanism is an ancient Greek hand-powered orrery, often
described as the oldest known analog computer. It was retrieved from the sea
in 1901 from a shipwreck off the coast of the Greek island of Antikythera,
and is dated to roughly 150–100 BC.

The device consists of at least 30 meshing bronze gears housed in a wooden
case roughly the size of a shoebox. By turning a hand crank, the user could
advance a pointer across a dial showing the position of the Sun and Moon in
the zodiac, the phase of the Moon, and the dates of solar and lunar eclipses
predicted by the Saros cycle. A separate dial tracked the four-year cycle of
the Olympiad and other Panhellenic games.

The mechanism's sophistication was not matched in the surviving record until
the astronomical clocks of medieval Europe, more than a thousand years later.
Its gear-cutting precision implies an established workshop tradition, yet no
comparable device or workshop has been found.

Derek de Solla Price published the first detailed scientific study in 1974,
using X-ray imaging to count gear teeth. The Antikythera Mechanism Research
Project, beginning in 2005, used microfocus X-ray computed tomography to read
inscriptions hidden inside the corroded fragments — effectively a user's
manual engraved on the device's plates. The inscriptions suggest the device
may have originated in Corinth or its colonies, possibly linking it to the
school of Archimedes in Syracuse.
